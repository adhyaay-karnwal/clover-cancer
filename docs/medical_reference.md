# Pancreatic Cancer Medical Reference

## Overview

Pancreatic cancer is the **3rd leading cause of cancer death** in the US, with a **5-year survival rate of ~12%**. It is projected to become the **2nd leading cause by 2030**.

### Why Early Detection Changes Everything

| Stage | 5-Year Survival | Median Survival |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Stage IA (localized, ≤2cm) | ~44% | >38 months |
| Stage IB (localized, >2cm) | ~37% | >30 months |
| Stage IIA (locally extensive, no nodes) | ~28% | ~24 months |
| Stage IIB (regional lymph nodes) | ~15% | ~18 months |
| Stage III (locally advanced) | ~8% | ~12 months |
| Stage IV (metastatic) | ~3% | ~6 months |

**Key insight:** Only ~10% of pancreatic cancers are caught at Stage I/II. Most are diagnosed at Stage III/IV.

## Risk Factors

### High Risk (Genetic/Familial)
- **BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations** — 2-6x increased risk
- **PALB2 mutations** — 3-4x increased risk
- **CDKN2A mutations** — 10-20x increased risk
- **STK11/LKB1 (Peutz-Jeghers syndrome)** — 130x increased risk
- **Lynch syndrome (MLH1, MSH2)** — 2-9x increased risk
- **Familial pancreatic cancer** — ≥2 first-degree relatives with PC = 6-32x risk

### Moderate Risk
- **Chronic pancreatitis** — 10-20x increased risk
- **New-onset diabetes (type 2)** — ~2x overall risk; specifically 0.625% of NOD patients ≥50 diagnosed with PC within 3 years (Chari et al., MD Anderson)
- **Long-standing diabetes (>5 years)** — 1.5-2x risk
- **Hereditary pancreatitis (PRSS1)** — 50-70x increased risk

#### New-Onset Diabetes as Early Marker (2025 Frontiers/PMC Research)
- ~1 in 160 (0.625%) new-onset diabetes patients ≥50 diagnosed with PC within 3 years (Chari et al.)
- Most PC diagnoses occur within first year of diabetes onset — suggesting cancer CAUSES diabetes
- ~50% of PC patients have diabetes at diagnosis; half are new-onset candidates
- Pancreatic cancer risk increased 50% in T2D diagnosis <4 years vs >5 years
- PC-associated diabetes is a distinct subtype of Type 3c (pancreatogenic) diabetes
- Oncogenic KRAS mutations (found in 90% of PC) drive tumor-related islet dysfunction
- Current prediction models achieve AUCs up to 0.91 for identifying high-risk NOD patients

### Lifestyle/Environmental
- **Smoking** — 2-3x increased risk (strongest modifiable risk factor)
- **Obesity (BMI >30)** — 1.5-2x increased risk
- **Heavy alcohol use** — 1.5-2x increased risk (especially with smoking)
- **Occupational exposure** — certain chemicals, pesticides, petroleum

### Age & Demographics
- **Age >60** — median age at diagnosis is 70
- **Slightly more common in men** (1.3:1 ratio)
- **African Americans** — higher incidence than other ethnic groups

## Symptoms & Presentation

### Classic Presentation (Often Late-Stage)
- **Jaundice** (painless) — most common presenting symptom of pancreatic head tumors
- **Abdominal pain** — dull, epigastric, radiating to back
- **Unintentional weight loss** — >10% body weight over 3-6 months
- **New-onset diabetes** — especially in patients >50 without typical risk factors

### Early/Subtle Symptoms (Often Dismissed)
These are the symptoms that get missed — the "diagnostic gap":

1. **New-onset diabetes or worsening glycemic control**
   - Studies show 0.5-1% of new-onset diabetics >50 will be diagnosed with PC within 3 years
   - Sudden loss of glycemic control in previously well-controlled diabetics
   - "Pancreatogenic diabetes" or Type 3c diabetes

2. **Unexplained back pain**
   - Dull, persistent, worse when lying down
   - Often attributed to musculoskeletal causes
   - May be the ONLY early symptom

3. **Abdominal discomfort/bloating**
   - Vague, epigastric
   - Often dismissed as GERD, IBS, or gastritis

4. **Steatorrhea (fatty stools)**
   - Pale, foul-smelling, floating stools
   - Indicates pancreatic enzyme insufficiency
   - Often overlooked

5. **Unexplained fatigue**
   - Non-specific but persistent

6. **Depression** (new-onset)
   - Often precedes cancer diagnosis
   - May be paraneoplastic

### Symptom Clusters That Should Raise Suspicion

| Cluster | Significance | Urgency |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| New-onset diabetes + weight loss + back pain | Classic early PC pattern | Urgent imaging |
| Painless jaundice + dark urine + weight loss | Likely pancreatic head mass | Emergency referral |
| New-onset diabetes + age >50 + no family history | PC screening consideration | Routine follow-up |
| Chronic pancreatitis + new symptoms | Cancer transformation risk | Urgent evaluation |
| BRCA2 mutation + new GI symptoms | High-risk genetic background | Urgent evaluation |
| Unexplained weight loss + fatigue + abdominal pain | Broad differential but PC must be ruled out | Urgent evaluation |

## Diagnostic Workup

### Imaging
1. **CT with contrast (pancreatic protocol)** — primary imaging modality
2. **MRI/MRCP** — alternative for small lesions, bile duct evaluation
3. **Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)** — most sensitive for small lesions, allows FNA biopsy
4. **PET-CT** — for staging, detecting metastases
5. **ERCP** — therapeutic (stenting) and diagnostic

### Biomarkers
- **CA 19-9** — most commonly used tumor marker
  - Sensitivity: 70-90%, Specificity: ~90%
  - Can be elevated in benign conditions (cholangitis, pancreatitis)
  - Useful for monitoring treatment response
- **CEA** — less specific, sometimes used in combination
- **KRAS mutations** — detectable in blood/pancreatic juice (emerging)

### When to Image (NCCN-Aligned Criteria)
- Any patient >50 with new-onset diabetes AND at least one of:
  - Unexplained weight loss
  - Abdominal/back pain
  - Elevated CA 19-9
  - Imaging abnormality
- Any patient with jaundice
- Any high-risk individual (BRCA2, Lynch, etc.) with new symptoms

## NCCN Screening Guidelines (High-Risk Individuals)

### Who Qualifies for Surveillance
- Known BRCA1/BRCA2, PALB2, CDKN2A mutations with ≥1 affected first-degree relative
- Peutz-Jeghers syndrome
- Hereditary pancreatitis
- ≥2 affected first-degree relatives with PC

### Recommended Surveillance
- Start at age 50 (or 10 years before youngest affected relative)
- Annual MRI/MRCP + EUS alternating
- CA 19-9 as adjunct

## Differential Diagnosis

When symptoms suggest pancreatic cancer, also consider:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Chronic pancreatitis | History of alcohol, calcifications on imaging |
| Ampullary adenoma | May present similarly, better prognosis |
| Cholangiocarcinoma | Bile duct origin, similar presentation |
| Pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor | Often functional (hormone-secreting), slower growing |
| Autoimmune pancreatitis | Elevated IgG4, responds to steroids |
| Duodenal cancer | May cause obstruction, different imaging |

## Key Statistics for Impact Narrative

- **~66,000** Americans diagnosed annually
- **~52,000** deaths annually
- **Only 10%** are candidates for surgery at diagnosis
- **71%** die within first year of diagnosis
- **Median time from symptom onset to diagnosis: 3-6 months** (often delayed)
- **New-onset diabetes precedes PC diagnosis in 0.5-1% of cases** — a missed screening opportunity

---

## Common Misdiagnoses

Pancreatic cancer is frequently mistaken for:
- Gallbladder disease
- Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
- Muscular pain
- Diverticulitis
- Chronic pancreatitis
- Autoimmune pancreatitis

**59% of misdiagnosed PC cases were initially reported as uncomplicated pancreatitis** (Kang et al., 2021).

## CT Findings Often Missed

From Johns Hopkins review (Ahmed et al., 2024):
- **38% of CT studies show pancreatic abnormalities up to 5 years before PC diagnosis**
- Small isoattenuating tumors without contour deformity
- Isolated pancreatic duct dilatation and cutoff
- Focal pancreatic enhancement and parenchymal atrophy
- Pancreatitis masking underlying PC
- Vascular encasement (subtle)
